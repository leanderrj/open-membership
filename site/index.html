Open Membership RSS
Protocol Compare Status GitHub

An open standard, draft 0.4 — feature-frozen, headed for IETF

Paid subscriptions on the web that belong to the web.

A small, namespaced extension to RSS that lets any publisher charge for content and any reader unlock it — without renting that relationship from Substack, Patreon, or a closed CMS. Three moving parts, eight conformance levels, MIT and CC-BY-4.0 throughout.

The protocol surface, in full

A publisher who adopts om adds a handful of namespaced elements to their existing RSS feed. That is the whole protocol surface a reader has to recognise to unlock paid content. 

<rss version="2.0" xmlns:om="http://purl.org/rss/modules/membership/">
  <channel>
    <title>Field Notes</title>
    <om:provider>https://fieldnotes.example</om:provider>
    <om:discovery>https://fieldnotes.example/.well-known/open-membership</om:discovery>

    <om:psp id="stripe" account="acct_..." />
    <om:tier id="paid" price="USD 8.00" period="monthly">Supporter</om:tier>

    <om:offer id="supporter-monthly" tier="paid">
      <om:price amount="8.00" currency="USD" period="P1M" />
      <om:checkout psp="stripe" price_id="price_supporter_..." />
    </om:offer>

    <item>
      <title>Members-only investigation: the warehouse fire</title>
      <om:access>locked</om:access>
      <om:preview>The fire investigators' report was filed two weeks before…</om:preview>
      <om:unlock>https://fieldnotes.example/unlock/2026-04-fire</om:unlock>
    </item>
  </channel>
</rss>

A reader that does not understand om keeps working unchanged; a reader that does, presents previews and unlock prompts. Compatibility-first, the way Podcasting 2.0 does it.

1. The feed

A few namespaced elements: provider, accepted PSPs, tiers, per-item access state. Nothing else changes.

2. The discovery document

One JSON document at .well-known/open-membership, composing with RFC 9728. Token endpoints, auth methods, revocation policy, privacy posture. One fetch, no negotiation.

3. The credential

A URL token, an OAuth bearer, or a W3C Verifiable Credential — depending on the publisher's conformance level. Portable across reader apps; export shape defined.

How it compares

The proprietary version of this already works. FeedPress and Outpost run paid RSS for Ghost; 404 Media and Aftermath are paying customers. What the open web is missing is interoperability — a feed shape, a discovery document, and a token contract any publisher can implement and any reader can read without negotiating with a vendor.

The Substack-paid-podcast model — per-subscriber tokenised RSS URLs that work in any podcast app — is the proof-of-concept that om's URL-token auth method works at scale. Substack already does it for millions of users. om standardises it.

Who it's for

Three publisher personas drive every design decision. They are not market segments; they are the test of whether a feature is actually pulling its weight.

The Substack writer who has migrated to Ghost

Escaped one walled garden, landed in a less walled one — but still no portable subscription mechanism. With om, the same Ghost instance emits a paid feed any RSS reader can consume. Drop in a plugin, your existing Stripe account keeps working, your subscribers stop being trapped in the Ghost web reader.

The Patreon podcaster expanding mediums

Patreon gives them per-tier URL-token feeds and a payment relationship; it does not give them text or video on the same terms. om keeps the per-tier URL-token mechanism they already use, adds the multi-medium support Patreon doesn't offer, and composes cleanly with Lightning value-splits via Podcasting 2.0.

The investigative or specialist publication that needs the privacy layer

A subscriber list that could be subpoenaed is a subscriber list a publication cannot ethically maintain. om's pseudonymous mode (OM-VC-SD with per-publisher unlinkable BBS+ pseudonyms) is built for them. If om reaches 1.0 with five such publications in production, it has accomplished something the incumbents cannot.

Conformance levels

Implementers commit to a level, the test suite verifies the level, and the level is what they advertise. Levels are cumulative.

LevelWhat it coversImplementer effort
1Parsing the namespace; previews; signup-URL displayone afternoon
2URL token auth; unlock endpoints; publisher-managed groups1–2 weeks
3OAuth bearer + DPoP; time-windowed access; SCIM groups2–4 weeks
4OM-VC (W3C Verifiable Credentials) presentation auth3–6 weeks
5Commerce: PSP declarations, offers, checkout, entitlements, gifts2–4 weeks
6Value-for-value: time-split recipients, Lightning composition2–4 weeks
7OM-VC-SD pseudonymous mode (BBS+ selective disclosure)4–8 weeks
8Bundle aggregation across publishers2–4 weeks

Most publishers and reader apps will live at Level 1, 2, or 5. Levels 6–8 are for the implementers who need them.

Status

  • Spec: feature-frozen at 0.4 draft. The next two revisions (0.5, 1.0) focus on conformance, interop, and governance — not new features.
  • Reference publishers: Ghost plugin and WordPress plugin, both targeting Level 5 (Commerce). Feature-complete; production-validated work in progress.
  • Reference reader: Miniflux fork scaffolded with a Go om/ module, fixture feeds, and an integration runbook.
  • Test suite: scaffolded with Level 1 tests live; Levels 2 and 5 stubbed.
  • Subscriber portability: spec drafted, round-trip harness implements the 26-test matrix.
  • IETF draft: draft-om-rss-00.md, 1,926 lines in kramdown-rfc2629 format, ready for the Independent Submission stream once the custodian is in place.
  • Custodian: four candidates in priority order — Internet Archive, Sovereign Tech Fund, NLnet Foundation, Software Freedom Conservancy. Outreach scheduled for Phase 3.

Eighteen months from spec draft to ratified open standard with multiple production deployments — see ROADMAP.md for the technical phasing.
